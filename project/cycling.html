Cycling

Pre-Olympics

At the time we came back from England and my father had his boarding house in Atjehstraat Katendrecht Holland. There was a fish-and-chips store on the opposite side of the street. I became friendly with the son of the owner of the store. He had a real racing bicycle. So I was allowed to have my sister’s old bike to ride on when he was finished peeling potatoes for his father to be used in the evening. One day he, Beb, told me he had won a race in his club of the juniors and his brother-in-law would buy him a new racing bike. So his old bike was for sale for 10 guilders. It did not take much effort for me to convince my parents to buy it for me. So now I could join the club and become a junior race cyclist.

You could see us always together on the race bikes after the potatoes were peeled. The first club race we had was a sprint, 300 meters from beginning to end. Believe it or not, I won and became junior sprint champion of our club “Fijenoord.” I seemed to have a natural acceleration at the start. Thereby, in the first 50 meters, I would be three lengths ahead before they could catch up to me. I would still be more than one length ahead at the end of the 300 meter mark.

This was not really good for my ego. I started training regularly and thought I could beat everyone. Well, I raced many long distance races and soon found out that to be a good contender, one must also go early to bed, rest well, eat and drink on time and keep concentrating on racing. However, with great pleasure and enjoyment, we had a wonderful time racing our bicycles in those years.

I have a book with articles and photos that Cathy so diligently kept up to date during my cycling career. I accomplished to win 16 sprint championships in different sections of the country.

Since the 1936 Olympics was coming up, I would be selected to be trained by the Dutch coach Mr. Gus Shilling (former world champion).

However, it was discovered that I could not take part in the Olympic team on account that my nationality was Chinese rather than Dutch, despite the fact that I was born and raised in Holland.

My Dutch license got revoked and all Dutch NWH racetracks were closed for me to race on. The Chinese ambassador Wunsz King, who had apparently heard of my dilemma, informed me that on his trip back to China, he would contact the Chinese Olympic committee in Shanghai. That is, if I would be willing to represent China in the Olympics as a cyclist.

After waiting for a long time, I finally received a letter from the Chinese Olympic Committee to fill in the blank form and return it immediately back to Shanghai, China.

Can you imagine? I think it was sent back the same day I received it. It did not take too long for them to send back the okay. I was in. Now it was up to me to put all my effort to do everything in my power to prove I was worth it to represent China.

1936 Berlin Olympics

In my first heat, I had an Italian “Rola” (1935 world champion). It is very usual that a champion gets an unknown opponent in his first heat, but for me it was a great disadvantage. Rola beat me by a half-length. The time for the last 300 meters was 13 seconds.

In the retrial heat, I excelled too late in the last curve to pass my Canadian champion “Peace” and lost by 30 c/m. [I raced] in a time that proved to be much too slow—15 seconds. For the last 200 meters, my tactics appeared to be not enough developed to ride against these experienced champions.

I further received an invitation to participate in the World Championship in 1937 Amsterdam, in Zurich Switzerland 1938, and in Paris France 1939. I just could not get myself placed into the quarter finals in either of those events. I seem to be weak in my insight of tactics.

I did not have any coaching. I was most of the time on my own and relied more on watching others—how they were taught to ride their races. Of course, one must have sprint abilities to be able to make 12 sec or less in the last 200 meters, but not necessarily can he always become the winner, and can even lose a sprint in 16 sec.

There is a tremendous lot of tactics involved. The greatest weapon one can have is to use your acceleration at the right moment by surprising the opponent either coming from behind him or accelerating down from the curve of the track when he leads his opponent in front.

1948 London Olympics

I started training again as I was eager to be selected again to represent China. Of course, there were no world championships or Olympics during the war. Unfortunately we missed 2 Olympics—1940 and 1944. So the 1948 Olympics in London was facing us besides getting ready for the world championship in Zurich Switzerland in 1946.

10th of June 1947: In that year I was again taking part in an international race in Zurich. During the race the public kept cheering every time I was passing them on the track. I had no idea why. When the race was over, I was told that they had received a call from Holland—that I had a boy son born. It was announced to the public by the loudspeaker. I do not know how I reacted, but I know I was laughing and crying the same time of joy.

I have forgotten to mention that during the Olympics 1948 in London, England, members of each country team that could speak English were selected to be invited for a tea-party in Buckingham Palace London with the English Royal Family.

When the athletes entered one by one into the large reception hall, a herald dressed in a beefeaters type outfit with a long staff in his hand made three knocks on the floor and, with a loud voice, he announced the name, country and type of sport we were participating in.

The royal family and other dignitaries were in one line. So, each of us shook their hands. Sometimes questions were asked and answered by us. Even the Queen and the Prince of Holland were lined up. Prince Bernard spoke to me and said that he had been informed about my nationality problem I had in Holland, and wished me good luck with my cycling event.
